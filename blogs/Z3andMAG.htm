Mining for Scientific Impact of Z3 using the Microsoft Academic Graph

 

 

Last week I was profoundly honored to receive the 2019 Herbrand Award at CADE 27 together with Leonardo de Moura in recognition of their numerous and important contributions to SMT solving, including its theory, implementation, and application to a wide range of academic and industrial needs. The Herbrand Award is given for distinguished contributions to the field of Automated Reasoning. Prior winners include luminaries in the field such as Robert Boyer, Martin Davies, J Strother Moore, Greg Nelson, and Alan Robinson. To say it mildly, it is truly humbling to be recognized in a field of giants. A similar characterization is due to peers in the SMT and automated reasoning community. It is a community that promotes an environment of shared standards and benchmarks. The SMT-LIB initiative and the TPTP library have without a doubt had a tremendous impact on advancing automated reasoning.

Herbrand Award Ceremony

The Herbrand Award ceremony includes a talk by the recipients. Leonardo and I prepared a joint talk that comprised of two main parts together with a reflection on the people who have shaped our careers in automated reasoning and its applications. In a first technical part it centered around model-constructing methods in Satisfiability Modulo Theories, SMT, solving. A second part examined areas where Z3, and more generally SMT, has had impact in the past decade. My daily routine revolves around user interactions on GitHub on issues, feature requests and general questions on how to use Z3. It provides a small glimpse into what users are doing with Z3, but it is all in the details and doesnt provide a high-level picture. Consequently, I turned to the Microsoft Academic Graph to extract information from scientific papers citing a tool paper on Z3. With more than 5000 citations since 2008 it can be expected to be well representative of SMT applications. This blog elaborates on how MAG can be queried and some of the extracted information.

Onboarding with MAG

First a disclaimer: this is my first use of MAG and I essentially never write SQL queries. My daily activities revolve around producing code, not much about analyzing data, so it was a fun opportunity for some hacking in an unfamiliar territory. Ramping up on MAG turned out to be a breeze, however. First, there is a set of excellent tutorials that illustrate using MAG from USQL and Python. As I often use Python I followed the basic MAG tutorial based on python and then used it as a starting point for my queries.

When following the graph of a paper it is useful to gain access to the unique identifier that MAG associates with the paper. To find this identifier, go to https://academic.microsoft.com/homeand search for the title of your favorite paper and follow the link to the MAG record. The identifier is listed in the URL that appears when you select the paper. For Z3, it looks as follows https://academic.microsoft.com/paper/1480909796/r...

Users

Z3 continues to be used by many in academia and industry today. It has helped ignite entire research disciplines such program synthesis and businesses such as MSRD. Tools powered by Z3 are used daily to make Azure reliable, establish verified cryptographic libraries and protocols, verify compiler optimizations, and for product line configurations to mention a few uses I am familiar with. I do not know about tools that schematize information about systems applications of Z3 so the information I have around commercial uses of Z3 is circumstantial and learned from interacting with users. At CADE, for instance, I learned about a new application domain from a participant, Aellison Cassimiro Teixeira Dos Santos, I happened to sit next to at lunch: he uses Z3 to compute a schedule that allows a re-transmission free network architecture for time sensitive networks to appear at FMCAD 2019. So yes, having fun with knowledge graphs is great, but nothing beats interacting directly with users.
